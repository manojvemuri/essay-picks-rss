Martin Picard’s Mitochondrial Theory of Mind

By Rachel Nuwer

A rigorous profile of the emerging idea that mitochondrial energy flow may connect cellular biology with health, mood, and conscious experience.

Core idea

Mitochondria may be much more than cellular power plants. They transform energy, produce signals, help regulate hormones and neurotransmitters, and vary across organs and cell types. Biologist Martin Picard argues that these organelles may provide a missing bridge between cellular metabolism, health, mood, stress, and perhaps consciousness.

Why it stands out

This is ambitious science writing with the brakes still attached. Nuwer gives Picard’s energetic theory its full imaginative force while making clear that its most dramatic claim — a mitochondrial basis for conscious experience — is not demonstrated. The evidence linking mitochondrial function to stress, behavior, and disease is increasingly substantial; the leap to consciousness remains a hypothesis. That distinction makes the piece useful rather than merely exciting.

The Ableism of Kings, the Fury of Trump

By Pasquale Toscano

A reading of Homer and contemporary politics that shows how leaders turn bodily difference into a weapon for discrediting rivals and dissenters.

Core idea

Reading Homer’s Odysseus alongside Donald Trump, Toscano examines how powerful men rhetorically “disable” opponents: they seize on bodily difference, convert it into supposed moral or intellectual weakness, and use public humiliation to make dissent appear illegitimate. The essay moves from Thersites and Polyphemus to Trump’s treatment of Serge Kovaleski and John McCain.

Why it stands out

The comparison is not a loose classical analogy. It identifies a repeatable political technique: authority strengthens itself by deciding whose body counts as evidence of inferiority. That lens complicates the familiar image of Odysseus as an ingenious everyman and makes ableism visible as a technology of rule, not merely a matter of offensive language.

The Problem AI Content Moderation Cannot Solve

By Hera Hussain

Why automated moderation misses harms rooted not in explicit content, but in consent, context, reputation, and culturally specific forms of abuse.

Core idea

Automated moderation can inspect what an image contains, but many serious harms depend on consent, intention, social context, and cultural meaning. A fully clothed photograph may be harmless in one setting and devastating when manipulated or redistributed to shame, blackmail, or endanger someone. Hussain argues that platforms need a consent-based framework and locally informed human moderation, not only better classifiers.

Why it stands out

This AI essay earns its place because it identifies a hard product limitation rather than gesturing at “bias” in general. Content is machine-readable; consent is relational. The piece also shows why moderation policies built around Western definitions of nudity and intimacy miss forms of image-based abuse affecting women in South Asia, the Middle East, and their diasporas.

The Heated Debate In Europe Over Air Conditioning

By Nathan Gardels

Europe’s discomfort with air conditioning reveals why climate mitigation and adaptation must be treated as complementary infrastructure strategies.

Core idea

Europe’s resistance to widespread air conditioning has become a proxy battle over technology, culture, energy use, and American-style consumption. Gardels argues that climate mitigation and adaptation are not competing agendas: decarbonizing energy and redesigning buildings must happen alongside practical protection from heat.

Why it stands out

This is shorter and more polemical than the other selections, but it offers a strategically useful correction. Infrastructure built for a previous climate cannot be preserved unchanged through moral preference alone. The real decision is not “natural versus artificial”; it is which cooling technologies, building standards, energy sources, and public rules can protect people without locking in additional emissions. Singapore serves as both an example of cooling-enabled development and a warning about fossil-fuel dependence.

Parapsychology Is Coming in From the Cold

By Lydia Wilson

A reported history of Cambridge’s psychical-research tradition and the uneasy boundary between consciousness studies, fringe inquiry, and mainstream science.

Core idea

Cambridge holds a substantial archive of psychical research and supports a little-known fellowship devoted to the field. Wilson traces the Society for Psychical Research from its 19th-century efforts to investigate telepathy, séances, and survival after death to contemporary researchers who hope that consciousness studies and physics might reopen questions long pushed to the margins.

Why it stands out

Its strength is institutional and historical, not evidentiary: the article does not establish that paranormal claims are true, and it notes that mainstream science remains broadly skeptical. What it does illuminate is how disciplines police their boundaries, how respected researchers pursue stigmatized questions, and how archives preserve failed, fraudulent, ambiguous, and possibly generative lines of inquiry together.

What a Picture of a Dog Has to Do with Reason and Logic

By Kristen French

Aphantasia challenges the old claim that abstract thought requires mental pictures, pointing instead toward a pluralist view of human cognition.

Core idea

David Hume argued that abstract concepts depend on specific mental images. People with aphantasia can reason about categories, mathematics, and other abstractions despite reporting no visual imagery, creating a serious challenge to that account. The deeper implication is not simply that Hume was wrong, but that different minds may reach the same cognitive destination through different internal strategies.

Why it stands out

This is a compact, unusually productive collision between philosophy and cognitive diversity. It does not oversell the evidence: the relevant neuroscience remains contested, some results have not yet been replicated, and the researchers leave room for nonvisual or unconscious processes. The durable payoff is a pluralist model of cognition—human beings may not all think in the same format.

Susanna Clarke: ‘I had been ill for 11 years. I felt like I was about to fall off the world’

By Susanna Clarke

Susanna Clarke reflects on chronic illness, the limits of language, and the stories sufferers use to make uncertainty livable.

Core idea

A century after Virginia Woolf’s On Being Ill, Clarke examines why sickness exceeds ordinary language and why sufferers nevertheless need stories to make it intelligible. Why it stands out

Its therapeutic discussion should be read as personal testimony rather than general medical guidance.

Same art, different name: would you see or hear it the same way?

By Rikki A Wolpowitz

A misattributed concerto reveals how reputation changes attention, interpretation, and even the pleasure we experience from art.

Core idea

A flute concerto by Leopold Hofmann was misattributed to Joseph Haydn for centuries. Once the attribution was corrected, the unchanged work appears to have lost status in teaching and performance. Wolpowitz uses the episode to explore prestige bias: reputation does not merely alter our stated opinion of art; it directs attention, grants ambiguity the benefit of the doubt, and may change the experience itself.

Why it stands out

The essay gives a precise name to something critics, managers, investors, and audiences do constantly. Its most useful concept is “interpretive sovereignty”: celebrated creators are assumed to be making intentional choices, while unknown creators must first prove that the same choices are not mistakes. That is a durable lens for understanding canons, brands, hiring, expertise, and status.

Thermodynamic Computers Go With the (Energy) Flow

By Philip Ball

A lucid look at computers that use thermal noise as a resource rather than an error source—and why the idea could radically reduce energy use.

Core idea

Conventional computers spend enormous effort suppressing thermal noise. Thermodynamic computing asks whether that noise can instead become a computational resource, using physical systems’ natural movement toward equilibrium—or their controlled nonequilibrium dynamics—to solve problems with potentially far less energy.

Why it stands out

This is not a breathless “new computer changes everything” story. Ball explains the physics, the competing architectures, and the important caveat: early prototypes and simulations are promising, but researchers have not yet demonstrated the decisive real-world energy advantage. The larger idea is durable and strange in the best way—computation may become more efficient by cooperating with matter rather than forcing matter to behave ideally.

Loitering towards war

Core idea

Why it stands out

Why Am I Left-Handed?

An intimate biological mystery links fetal movement, spinal-cord asymmetry, microtubules, evolution, and humanity’s unusual bias toward the right hand.

Core idea

Handedness may begin before the brain is connected to the limbs. Fetal arm preference appears to emerge from early spinal-cord asymmetry, while variants across dozens of genes—many involving microtubules—may tilt development without determining it. That still leaves the largest question unresolved: why humans, unlike other primates, show such a strong 90–10 population bias toward right-handedness.

Why it stands out

A familiar personal trait becomes a genuinely multiscale scientific mystery spanning embryology, genetics, neuroscience, evolution, and culture. Wolchover does not force a neat answer; she distinguishes established findings from plausible mechanisms and speculative evolutionary explanations. It is the strongest combination here of wonder, clarity, and intellectual honesty.

We Know Simple Fluids Can Flow. Turns Out, Some Can Fracture.

A simple nonelastic fluid cracked like a brittle solid, challenging a basic assumption about how liquids fracture.

Core idea

A nonelastic, apparently simple hydrocarbon fluid can crack like brittle glass under rapid stress. That observation undermines the familiar assumption that elasticity is what allows a fluid to fracture and revives a deeper possibility: fracture may arise from molecular cohesion, cavitation, and loading rate rather than elasticity alone.

Why it stands out

This is compact science writing at its best: an audible laboratory surprise leads into a genuine conceptual problem, then outward to unresolved theory and possible applications in fiber spinning, inkjet printing, brain-injury protection, and soft robotics. The result is more than a novelty report. It shows science correcting its categories when nature refuses to respect them.
