‘Spermageddon’: is the world facing a male reproductive crisis?

By Hannah Devlin

A careful investigation of falling sperm counts, testosterone, metabolic health, pollution claims, and what the evidence does—and does not—show.

Core idea

Claims of a collapse in male reproductive health combine several different questions: whether sperm counts and testosterone have fallen, how much study methods explain the apparent trends, which environmental exposures matter, and how strongly metabolic health contributes. Devlin maps the evidence and the disagreements rather than presenting “Spermageddon” as an established diagnosis.

Why it stands out

This is excellent science journalism because it resists both complacency and panic. The strongest sections show how a politically charged story can outrun the reliability of the measurements beneath it. The article also distinguishes plausible concern from premature certainty: obesity, diabetes, and wider metabolic health have clearer links to male reproductive function than many sweeping claims about microplastics or an inevitable species-wide collapse. Its warning about indiscriminate testosterone treatment is especially useful because supplemental testosterone can suppress sperm production.

The U.S. wants to contain China’s AI. Silicon Valley keeps using it

By Paul Triolo

Chinese open-weight models are becoming inputs to American AI development, revealing the limits of technology containment and the strategic power of ecosystems.

Core idea

The deeper lesson is that ecosystem adoption can become geopolitical power: a model that others find useful may shape global development even when its home country lacks control over the downstream applications.

The Protests Gripping India — and Rattling Modi

By Surbhi Gupta

How exam failures, youth precarity, satire, and state repression are turning a narrow grievance into a wider challenge to political legitimacy.

Core idea

A movement that began around exam irregularities, student suicides, and frustration with educational administration has broadened into a more general expression of youth precarity and political anger. Gupta describes how street mobilization, online satire, internet restrictions, and the treatment of activist Sonam Wangchuk have turned a bureaucratic grievance into a test of the government’s legitimacy.

Why it stands out

The piece also shows humor and meme culture functioning not as political decoration but as tools for lowering fear and coordinating dissent. It is an argued interpretation of a fast-moving movement, not a neutral final account, which is worth keeping in mind.

The savage bites back

By Sofia Cándano

How Brazilian modernists turned the colonial fantasy of cannibalism into a theory of cultural agency: absorb, transform, and return borrowed forms on your own terms.

Core idea

Brazilian modernists transformed the colonial stereotype of the cannibal into “anthropophagy,” a theory of cultural creation. Instead of choosing between copying European culture and rejecting it in favor of an imagined purity, artists such as Oswald de Andrade and Tarsila do Amaral proposed digesting outside influences, transforming them, and returning something unmistakably their own.

Why it stands out

This is more than an art-history essay. It provides a powerful model for creative borrowing: agency comes not from avoiding influence but from metabolizing it. The framework also unsettles simple stories about authenticity and appropriation, showing how a stigmatizing colonial image can be seized and converted into a method of resistance. The essay’s reach beyond Brazil makes the concept feel less like a historical curiosity and more like a living theory of cultural production.

Blindsighted

By Leo Kim

A critique of Neuralink’s vision-restoration ambitions and the ideology that turns disability, perception, and human augmentation into engineering abstractions.

Core idea

Neuralink’s proposed “Blindsight” implant is presented as a path from restoring vision to eventually producing forms of sight beyond ordinary human capability. Kim uses that ambition to examine a broader ideology in which perception is reduced to data transmission, disability becomes an engineering deficit, and therapeutic work slides quickly into fantasies of enhancement.

Why it stands out

The essay asks a question that technological roadmaps often suppress: whose account of a problem determines what counts as a solution? Restoring useful vision, respecting disabled people’s lived experience, and pursuing “superhuman” perception are not the same objective. The piece is polemical cultural criticism rather than a technical feasibility review, but that is precisely its value—it makes the assumptions surrounding the engineering project visible.

The Organisms That Make Earth’s Harshest Places Home

By Jake Buehler

A visual tour of extremophiles—and what their chemistry reveals about biotechnology, life’s origins, and possible life beyond Earth.

Core idea

Life has colonized environments that seem chemically or physically impossible: boiling springs, frozen deserts, battery-acid conditions, crushing ocean pressure, toxic metal deposits, and intense radiation. These extremophiles are not merely curiosities. Their enzymes and survival strategies already support molecular biology and may help with industrial chemistry, pollution cleanup, research on life’s origins, and the search for life beyond Earth.

Why it stands out

This is a visual gallery rather than a conventional long essay, but it earns its place through density and range. The most useful lesson is that basic biological curiosity can become foundational technology: the heat-stable enzyme isolated from Thermus aquaticus, for example, enabled PCR and transformed modern genetics. It is also a disciplined way to think about alien life—start by expanding the known limits of terrestrial life rather than inventing extraterrestrial organisms from scratch.

What Humanity Needs To Flourish In The Next Decade

By E. Glen Weyl, James Evans, and Chris White

A serious agenda for “reverse alignment”: redesigning institutions, laws, skills, and governance so society can absorb AI without sacrificing prosperity or accountability.

Core idea

AI alignment is only half the problem. Even a well-behaved system can produce broad harm or disappointing gains when introduced into institutions that cannot verify its output, distribute its benefits, constrain concentrated power, or help people adapt. The authors call the neglected half “reverse alignment”: redesigning laws, organizations, skills, civic systems, and norms so society can absorb powerful technology productively.

Why it stands out

This AI essay earns its slot because it moves from alarm to institutional agenda. Its three failure modes—productivity without prosperity, execution without verification, and capacity without constraint—are strong diagnostic tools for executives and policymakers. It also offers concrete areas for work, including privacy-preserving identity, provenance, data compensation, agent trust, workplace redesign, democratic participation, education, and labor transition. Read it as an ambitious agenda-setting manifesto rather than neutral evidence: two authors are Microsoft Research leaders, and the essay reflects a coalition’s proposed program.

‘She was almost a Batman figure’: Mexico’s avenging Mother Courage

By Sam Edwards

How a grieving mother became a national anti-crime hero—and how coercion, weak evidence, media power, and institutional capture complicated the case she built.

Core idea

After her son Hugo disappeared in 2005, Isabel Miranda de Wallace became Mexico’s most celebrated anti-kidnapping campaigner, using private investigators, billboards, political access, and relentless media pressure to identify alleged perpetrators. Edwards reconstructs how that heroic narrative became entangled with coerced confessions, torture allegations, questionable forensic evidence, institutional patronage, and prolonged imprisonment.

Why it stands out

This is the strongest original reporting in today’s set. It shows how a compelling public story can acquire enough political and media force to shape what investigators, courts, and audiences are willing to see. In 2025, Mexico’s supreme court ordered one defendant released after nearly 20 years, finding that her confession was obtained through torture and that no direct evidence linked her to the disappearance. The article avoids an easy reversal in which one hero simply becomes one villain; its deeper subject is how institutions lose the ability to correct a story once too many powerful reputations depend on it.

Elite Young Runners Are Becoming Freakishly Fast. Welcome to ‘Trackflation’

By Brendan I. Koerner

Youth running’s performance boom reveals how coaching, technology, money, social media, and escalating expectations can raise achievement while increasing risk.

Core idea

American school-age runners are becoming dramatically faster because an entire performance ecosystem has intensified: younger specialization, private clubs and coaches, professional training methods, carbon-plated shoes, supplements, national meets, social media, sponsorships, and increasingly precise data. The same system that raises the ceiling also raises the minimum expected performance.

Why it stands out

This is not merely a sports trend story. It is a case study in what happens when optimization tools diffuse downward into childhood. Objective measurement makes track feel meritocratic, but money buys coaching, travel, equipment, and access to fast meets; social media turns teenagers into brands; and escalating standards can produce burnout, injury, delayed puberty, anxiety, and incentives for prohibited substances. The durable question is applicable well beyond athletics: when everyone gains better tools, do people become freer—or does the baseline simply become harder to survive?

When the world stopped moving

By Devin A Giordano

An incarcerated writer describes learning to notice the small space between impulse and reaction without romanticizing the conditions that forced the lesson.

Core idea

Giordano describes entering prison at 19 while detoxing and accustomed to using noise, drugs, anger, and constant motion to avoid himself. Years of enforced stillness gradually taught him to observe fear and anger before acting—to notice a small interval between impulse and response in which choice remains possible.

Why it stands out

The essay offers personal growth without pretending that incarceration is a wellness retreat. Giordano does not claim serenity, redemption, or a shortened sentence; he describes awareness built under coercive and often frightening conditions. Its most transferable insight is that motion can masquerade as meaning, while self-knowledge begins when distraction no longer automatically converts discomfort into action.

Transit of wonder

By Ananya Palivela

A history of the transit of Venus and the astronomers who crossed the world to turn a fleeting shadow into a ruler for the solar system.

Core idea

The transit of Venus is a tiny visual event with an enormous scientific history. By timing the planet’s passage across the Sun from widely separated points on Earth, 18th- and 19th-century astronomers could use parallax to estimate the scale of the solar system. Their expeditions joined mathematical ingenuity and genuine awe to danger, rivalry, empire, and occasionally ruin.

Why it stands out

Palivela makes measurement feel human. The essay explains the geometry clearly, but its deeper subject is why people will organize years of life around a few minutes of uncertain observation. It also refuses a sanitized heroic history: the global scientific network that made the measurements possible was entangled with imperial reach.

What Makes Humans Stupid

By David C. Krakauer

David Krakauer argues that stupidity is not low intelligence but intelligence elaborating, defending, and institutionalizing error.

Core idea

Stupidity is not simply the absence of intelligence. Krakauer defines it as the sophisticated elaboration of error: using language, theory, technology, ideology, and institutions to make an easy problem harder, defend a bad answer, and build further action on top of it. On this account, the capacity for consequential stupidity grows with the machinery available for reasoning.

Why it stands out

The distinction between ignorance, ordinary error, and institutionalized error is genuinely useful. It explains why expertise and complexity do not automatically protect organizations from failure—and can sometimes make failure more durable. The essay is deliberately sweeping, but it offers a strong diagnostic question: is added sophistication reducing the problem, or merely making the wrong answer harder to abandon?
