What a Picture of a Dog Has to Do with Reason and Logic

By Kristen French

Aphantasia challenges the old claim that abstract thought requires mental pictures, pointing instead toward a pluralist view of human cognition.

Core idea

David Hume argued that abstract concepts depend on specific mental images. People with aphantasia can reason about categories, mathematics, and other abstractions despite reporting no visual imagery, creating a serious challenge to that account. The deeper implication is not simply that Hume was wrong, but that different minds may reach the same cognitive destination through different internal strategies.

Why it stands out

This is a compact, unusually productive collision between philosophy and cognitive diversity. It does not oversell the evidence: the relevant neuroscience remains contested, some results have not yet been replicated, and the researchers leave room for nonvisual or unconscious processes. The durable payoff is a pluralist model of cognition—human beings may not all think in the same format.

Susanna Clarke: ‘I had been ill for 11 years. I felt like I was about to fall off the world’

By Susanna Clarke

Susanna Clarke reflects on chronic illness, the limits of language, and the stories sufferers use to make uncertainty livable.

Core idea

A century after Virginia Woolf’s On Being Ill, Clarke examines why sickness exceeds ordinary language and why sufferers nevertheless need stories to make it intelligible. A narrative can organize uncertainty, restore a measure of agency, and shape the experience of both illness and recovery—even when it cannot establish a definitive medical cause.

Why it stands out

Clarke writes from inside chronic illness without converting suffering into a tidy lesson or resilience performance. The essay is strongest when it distinguishes bodily experience from the explanations imposed on it and when it shows how many incompatible stories a skilled writer can generate from the same life. Its therapeutic discussion should be read as personal testimony rather than general medical guidance.

India’s crackdown on a new WhatsApp feature risks setting a global precedent

By Ananya Bhattacharya

India’s objection to WhatsApp usernames shows how one large market can reshape privacy architecture and product standards worldwide.

Core idea

India has challenged WhatsApp’s planned username feature on cyberfraud grounds. Because product architecture, privacy safeguards, and contact discovery cannot always be changed cleanly market by market, a concession in one major jurisdiction could encourage similar demands elsewhere and gradually reshape a global communications product.

Why it stands out

This is a sharp case study in regulatory leverage. It moves beyond the familiar encryption debate to show how governments can influence technology by contesting individual design features before demonstrated harm occurs. The strategic lesson is broader than WhatsApp: a sufficiently important market can turn a local policy demand into a worldwide product constraint.

Same art, different name: would you see or hear it the same way?

By Rikki A Wolpowitz

A misattributed concerto reveals how reputation changes attention, interpretation, and even the pleasure we experience from art.

Core idea

A flute concerto by Leopold Hofmann was misattributed to Joseph Haydn for centuries. Once the attribution was corrected, the unchanged work appears to have lost status in teaching and performance. Wolpowitz uses the episode to explore prestige bias: reputation does not merely alter our stated opinion of art; it directs attention, grants ambiguity the benefit of the doubt, and may change the experience itself.

Why it stands out

The essay gives a precise name to something critics, managers, investors, and audiences do constantly. Its most useful concept is “interpretive sovereignty”: celebrated creators are assumed to be making intentional choices, while unknown creators must first prove that the same choices are not mistakes. That is a durable lens for understanding canons, brands, hiring, expertise, and status.

It’s Not Easy Being Green

By Rob Arcand

A searching account of music’s environmental footprint and why easy green fixes can conceal the industry’s deeper structures and tradeoffs.

Core idea

Reviewing Kyle Devine’s Recomposed, Arcand examines the full environmental system behind music: physical formats, streaming infrastructure, artist travel, audience travel, supply chains, and climate accounting. Many apparently green interventions improve one visible metric while leaving the industry’s deeper consumption patterns and unequal production system intact.

Why it stands out

This is a strong antidote to both personal-footprint moralism and vague declarations that “capitalism is the problem.” It asks where measurement boundaries are drawn, which emissions remain invisible, and whether a solution changes the system or merely makes its current form easier to defend. The result is a useful strategy essay about externalities disguised as music criticism.
