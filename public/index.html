China’s AI talent race is starting in high school

By Kinling Lo

Chinese technology companies are extending AI talent pipelines into secondary school, challenging conventional assumptions about credentials, training, and when firms should identify exceptional ability.

Core idea

Chinese technology companies are expanding AI talent pipelines from universities into secondary school. Tencent is running camps for teenagers, a ByteDance-linked research centre selects 16- to 18-year-olds for intensive work, and Geely now offers some school-leavers company-designed training plus a guaranteed job. The underlying bet is that in a fast-changing field, demonstrated ability and learning velocity may matter more than age, degree, or conventional work experience.

Why it stands out

This is AI reporting about organizational strategy rather than model spectacle. It shows firms responding to scarce talent by changing when they recruit, what counts as evidence of ability, and how tightly education is linked to employment. The piece also preserves the central tension: an early opportunity for an unusually capable teenager can easily become premature professionalization, surveillance, or pressure imposed on a child.

‘Most people don’t know we’ve set up a doomsday system’: physicist Laura Grego on satellites, nuclear weapons and the battle for our skies

By Aida Edemariam

Physicist Laura Grego explains how military dependence, commercial satellite expansion, fragile treaties, and compressed nuclear decision times are turning space into critical and dangerously under-governed infrastructure.

Core idea

Space is no longer mainly a realm of exploration. Satellites now underpin communications, navigation, commerce, warfare, intelligence, and nuclear command, while commercial constellations and military counter-space capabilities are expanding faster than law or public debate. Grego’s starkest point is that existing nuclear systems can force a president to make civilization-scale decisions in minutes, under uncertainty, because land-based missiles are operated on a “use them or lose them” logic.

Why it stands out

The interview connects several subjects normally treated separately: nuclear deterrence, satellite infrastructure, private ownership, space law, AI-assisted military speed, orbital debris, and the privatization of the night sky. Its durable lesson is that technological systems create facts and dependencies long before democratic institutions decide what rules should govern them.

How the War in Ukraine Reshaped Israel’s ‘Russian’ Community

By Nikita Aronov

A reported account of how war, migration, language, and generational change fractured Israel’s once-monolithic “Russian” identity into competing political communities.

Core idea

Russia’s invasion of Ukraine fractured an Israeli population commonly treated as one “Russian-speaking” bloc. Language, migration wave, generation, relationship to Putin, Ukrainian identity, Israeli politics, and the Gaza war now pull that population in different directions. A label that once appeared socially and electorally coherent has become a set of competing identities.

Why it stands out

This is unusually textured reporting about how geopolitical shocks reorganize identity from the inside. It shows that assimilation does not simply erase older affiliations; a new war can reactivate, divide, or reinvent them. The most transferable insight is that demographic categories remain politically useful long after they stop describing a real community.

What ancient art tells us about modern psychedelic medicine

By William McCarthy

Lower Pecos rock art becomes a prompt to ask whether psychedelic medicine loses therapeutic value when it isolates molecules from ritual, place, story, and community.

Core idea

Lower Pecos rock paintings and archaeological evidence of ancient peyote use suggest that psychedelic healing was embedded in place, ritual, story, community, and ecological relationship. McCarthy argues that modern clinical research may lose part of the therapeutic mechanism when it treats psychedelics only as isolated molecules acting on individual brains.

Why it stands out

The essay makes a useful challenge to pharmaceutical reductionism: treatment context is not merely decoration around a chemical intervention. It also links archaeology, Indigenous knowledge, neuroscience, cancer care, and environmental connection in a way that is genuinely generative. Read it with one caveat: continuity between ancient imagery and present clinical practice is interpretive, and the murals do not themselves prove which elements improve medical outcomes.

The Paths Forward for the Gulf States and Iran

By Kamran Bokhari

Why Gulf governments are likely to manage rather than resolve their conflict with Iran, balancing deterrence, diplomacy, economic exposure, and dependence on outside security powers.

Core idea

Gulf governments are unlikely to resolve their conflict with Iran. Their more realistic objective is continuing management: deter missiles and proxies, keep channels to Tehran open, preserve energy and trade flows, rely on outside security partners, and avoid becoming the battlefield in someone else’s confrontation.

Why it stands out

Although anchored in a fast-moving war, the essay offers a durable strategic framework. The Gulf states face a classic exposure problem: they want Iranian power constrained, but they bear a disproportionate share of the economic and physical cost when constraint becomes open war. “Management rather than resolution” is a useful lens for many conflicts in which no actor can impose a stable settlement at acceptable cost.
