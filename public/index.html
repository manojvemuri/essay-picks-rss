Thermodynamic Computers Go With the (Energy) Flow

By Philip Ball

A lucid look at computers that use thermal noise as a resource rather than an error source—and why the idea could radically reduce energy use.

Core idea

Conventional computers spend enormous effort suppressing thermal noise. Thermodynamic computing asks whether that noise can instead become a computational resource, using physical systems’ natural movement toward equilibrium—or their controlled nonequilibrium dynamics—to solve problems with potentially far less energy.

Why it stands out

This is not a breathless “new computer changes everything” story. Ball explains the physics, the competing architectures, and the important caveat: early prototypes and simulations are promising, but researchers have not yet demonstrated the decisive real-world energy advantage. The larger idea is durable and strange in the best way—computation may become more efficient by cooperating with matter rather than forcing matter to behave ideally.

Loitering towards war

By Anton Cebalo

A history of how Vienna’s coffeehouses nurtured modernism while Belgrade’s kafanas incubated nationalist conspiracy and revolutionary violence.

Core idea

Vienna’s coffeehouses became incubators for modernist art and intellectual life, while Belgrade’s kafanas helped revolutionary nationalism and conspiratorial networks take shape. Similar social institutions—cheap places to linger, argue, and form identities—produced radically different cultural and political outcomes.

Why it stands out

The essay complicates fashionable praise for “third places.” Social spaces do not automatically produce tolerance or civic health; they intensify whatever networks, grievances, ambitions, and myths circulate within them. Cebalo connects architecture, leisure, literature, nationalism, and the assassination of Franz Ferdinand without reducing history to a single cause.

The Dacre dynasty: how Britain’s rightwing press was radicalised

By Andy Beckett

How Paul Dacre’s newsroom methods and protégés spread outrage-driven culture-war journalism across Britain’s nominally competing rightwing newspapers.

Core idea

Paul Dacre’s influence on British journalism extends well beyond his long editorship of the Daily Mail. Beckett traces how editors and executives trained in Dacre’s newsroom carried its top-down management, outrage-driven framing, and culture-war instincts into the Telegraph, Times, Sunday Times, and other institutions.

Why it stands out

This is unusually strong institutional reporting. It shows that ideological change can spread through personnel pipelines and operating culture, not only through ownership or audience demand. Nominally competing newspapers can become more alike because the same managerial school supplies their leaders. Beckett also asks whether this model can survive fragmented digital audiences and generational change rather than treating its dominance as permanent.

The Gulf has billions to spend on AI. It still needs Nvidia

By Indranil Ghosh

Why Gulf states’ vast AI budgets cannot overcome Nvidia’s hardware lead, CUDA lock-in, and the geopolitical constraints attached to advanced chips.

Core idea

Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates can commit tens of billions of dollars to AI infrastructure, but capital alone cannot free them from Nvidia. The company’s hardware lead, CUDA software ecosystem, and the export controls surrounding advanced chips create a dependency that regional diversification efforts cannot quickly overcome.

Why it stands out

This AI piece earns its slot because it is about a concrete strategic constraint rather than generic transformation. It distinguishes frontier-model training from narrower inference workloads, where alternatives may have more room, and shows how vendor lock-in and geopolitical permission can matter more than purchasing power. The lesson travels well beyond chips: an ecosystem is a deeper moat than a product.

Unmothered

By David Robson

A science-informed memoir about how parental estrangement can fracture autobiographical memory—and why our identities are partly co-authored by other people.

Core idea

Robson uses estrangement from his mother to examine how autobiographical memory depends on other people. Parents and close relationships do not merely witness our lives; they help retrieve, interpret, and stabilize the stories through which we understand who we are.

Why it stands out

This is personal writing strengthened by cognitive science rather than decorated with it. The essay challenges the self-help assumption that identity is an entirely private construction. Its most useful insight is that revising a relationship can alter the remembered past, sometimes causing every earlier event to be rewritten as inevitable foreshadowing. Robson argues for living with contradiction instead of forcing a perfectly coherent life story.
