Transit of wonder

By Ananya Palivela

A history of the transit of Venus and the astronomers who crossed the world to turn a fleeting shadow into a ruler for the solar system.

Core idea

The transit of Venus is a tiny visual event with an enormous scientific history. By timing the planet’s passage across the Sun from widely separated points on Earth, 18th- and 19th-century astronomers could use parallax to estimate the scale of the solar system. Their expeditions joined mathematical ingenuity and genuine awe to danger, rivalry, empire, and occasionally ruin.

Why it stands out

Palivela makes measurement feel human. The essay explains the geometry clearly, but its deeper subject is why people will organize years of life around a few minutes of uncertain observation. It also refuses a sanitized heroic history: the global scientific network that made the measurements possible was entangled with imperial reach.

What Makes Humans Stupid

By David C. Krakauer

David Krakauer argues that stupidity is not low intelligence but intelligence elaborating, defending, and institutionalizing error.

Core idea

Stupidity is not simply the absence of intelligence. Krakauer defines it as the sophisticated elaboration of error: using language, theory, technology, ideology, and institutions to make an easy problem harder, defend a bad answer, and build further action on top of it. On this account, the capacity for consequential stupidity grows with the machinery available for reasoning.

Why it stands out

The distinction between ignorance, ordinary error, and institutionalized error is genuinely useful. It explains why expertise and complexity do not automatically protect organizations from failure—and can sometimes make failure more durable. The essay is deliberately sweeping, but it offers a strong diagnostic question: is added sophistication reducing the problem, or merely making the wrong answer harder to abandon?

Skill nostalgia

By Joshua Habgood-Coote

Why the longing for handmade work can be escapist nostalgia—or a serious critique of work stripped of skill, autonomy, and meaning.

Core idea

The renewed appetite for baking, pottery, beekeeping, repair, and other handmade practices is partly a response to work that feels abstract, fragmented, or vulnerable to automation. Habgood-Coote asks whether this longing is merely an idealized retreat into the past or whether it can help recover autonomy, judgment, beauty, and social meaning in future work.

Why it stands out

The essay neither romanticizes artisanal labor nor dismisses craft culture as lifestyle consumption. It treats nostalgia as politically ambiguous: it can hide exploitation and feed reactionary fantasies, but it can also preserve an awareness of what industrial and digital systems have taken away. That makes it a better guide than the usual “quit your laptop job and make things” argument.

Mesa-Optimization is Destroying Education

By Matt Duffy

How Campbell’s Law and mesa-optimization explain schools that maximize grades and credentials while losing sight of actual learning.

Core idea

Duffy combines Campbell’s Law with the AI-alignment concept of mesa-optimization. Institutions begin with a real objective—such as learning—but create subordinate systems that optimize proxies such as grades, test performance, admissions outcomes, and compliance. Over time, those proxy optimizers can become effective at surviving and scoring while drifting away from the original purpose.

Why it stands out

The terminology is technical, but the institutional diagnosis is sharp and portable. The piece explains how schools can produce impressive records alongside weak mastery, and why simply adding another metric rarely repairs the problem. Its discussion of AI is unusually useful: AI is not presented only as a cheating tool or miracle tutor, but as both an accelerant of proxy gaming and a possible aid to richer, more transparent assessment.

Pakistan’s Rise To A Middle Power

By Bina Shah

Bina Shah argues that Pakistan’s brokerage among rival powers is elevating it into a more consequential middle-power role.

Core idea

Shah argues that Pakistan is converting its relationships with the United States, China, Gulf states, Iran, and Afghanistan into a more consequential middle-power role. Its value lies less in commanding a bloc than in brokering among rivals, supplying military and diplomatic capabilities, and remaining useful to powers that otherwise share few interests.

Why it stands out

The essay provides a useful lens for a more fragmented world order: countries can gain leverage through strategic position, mediation, and network centrality even when they lack superpower-scale wealth. It should be read as an argued interpretation rather than a settled verdict—Pakistan’s domestic weakness and security problems remain substantial—but the middle-power framework is more durable than the immediate news hook.
